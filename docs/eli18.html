eigenorg, explained like you're 18

Why adding people (or AI) to a messy org makes it worse

This page explains, in plain words, what the eigenorg simulator actually computes — and shows the real calibration curves the model produces. It is a thinking aid grounded in research, not a prediction engine: it gets the shapes and directions right from published research, while many exact numbers are editorial defaults you can adjust.

1 · How the clock works

The simulator moves in steps of one working day. A default run is 60 steps — about 12 working weeks. Because real orgs are noisy, every run is repeated 500 times with different random draws (a Monte Carlo simulation), and the charts show three lines per metric: the middle of all runs (p50) and a shaded band from the luckiest 10% to the unluckiest 10% (p10–p90). If a band is wide, the outcome is genuinely uncertain. If two bands don't overlap, the difference is not noise.

3 · The words on the charts

coordination tax
The slice of everyone's day spent keeping people aligned instead of producing — meetings, handoffs, waiting. It grows with the square of team size (12 people = 66 possible conversations; 25 people = 300), which is why the tax explodes quietly. Meeting-heavy cultures pay ~1.5× more for the same structure.
decision latency
Working days from "someone proposed this" to "someone with authority said yes". Each approval layer adds ~2–3 days plus queueing.
WIP
Work In Progress: tasks started but not finished — including everything stuck waiting for an approval.
cohesion
The human glue (0–100): trust, shared context, people flagging problems early. It erodes when teams outgrow ~15 people, when more teammates are AI agents, and when the person people actually talked to is replaced by a router.
brittleness
Events where an AI agent hits a case beyond its ceiling — almost always a novel task. If nobody owns the recovery, everything nearby runs 1.5–2.5× slower for the next 3–5 days.
structural health
A 1–10 score defined by five yes-ish/no-ish questions: Is there a single owner for cross-team work? Do teams finish before starting new work? Can a routine decision happen without a meeting? Do people know what they may decide alone? Is there a named owner when something novel breaks?
entropy
A 0–100 summary index blending coordination tax, decision latency, frayed cohesion, brittleness, and piled-up WIP. 0 = crisp org, 100 = thrash. It is deliberately the secondary metric — headline numbers stay in days, %, and items/day.

4 · Five stories the model must get right

These five scenarios are the model's exam. Each curve below is a real output of the calibration harness — 500 Monte Carlo runs on the published default coefficients — and each claim is enforced by automated golden tests, so the shipped engine cannot quietly drift away from what you see here.

Story 1 — The Coordination Collapse

A 12-person team doubles to 25 in six months. No structure is added; the culture is meeting-heavy. More hands means more output at first — then the quadratic channel math takes over: every new person adds more conversations than capacity.

Story 2 — The Three-Layer Prioritization Tax

Same 20-person org, same work — the only difference is whether an idea needs one approval or three. Each layer sits on a decision for 2–3 days, has less bandwidth than the layer below it, and sometimes overrides work already in flight (which re-enters the queue keeping only half its progress).

Story 4 — The Dunbar Cliff

A pod-based org grows from 3 pods (24 people) to 8 pods (64) with no structural change. Every pod stays small, tight, healthy. The org as a whole does not.

Story 5 — The Hollow Middle

A team replaces its human PM with an AI prioritization agent. Front-line engineers and the exec layer stay human. On paper the AI covers prioritization better — and for routine routing, it truly does.

Plus one — the prioritization configurator

The app lets you stack 1–5 approval layers and pick who sits in each seat: a human PM, a human director, an AI prioritization agent, or a committee. A sixth test scenario locks the behavior that keeps this honest. Take a 3-layer stack and swap only the middle seat from a human PM to an AI agent: settled routine decision latency drops to about 0.87× (the agent clears routine approvals fast and adds bandwidth) — but novel-task brittleness rises from zero to ~4 events over the run, because a novel case now hits an AI router with no human judgment behind it. Same speed-up, same trap as the Hollow Middle, now as a knob you can turn. Both numbers are golden-tested at the default coefficients.

5 · How honest is this model?

What the model is NOT: a prediction of your org. No simulation this simple can be. Outputs are ranges under stated assumptions, full stop.

What it IS: the well-supported shapes of organizational physics, wired together and made explorable. Each label says where the number itself comes from — not just the direction of the effect:

  • peer-reviewed — the number (not merely the direction) comes from peer-reviewed work. Honest disclosure: zero coefficient values in v1 qualify. Several mechanism directions rest on peer-reviewed work (Brooks' channel math, 1975; Conway's mirroring, empirically confirmed by Harvard/Microsoft studies), but no coefficient value does — and the drawer says so per number.
  • industry-report — the number comes from named industry research or established practice (2–3 days per approval layer; the ~5/15/50/150 cognitive bands — which are themselves contested, and the model says so).
  • editorial-heuristic — an adjustable default we chose so the mechanic behaves plausibly; the direction may still be well-supported even when the magnitude is ours. This includes the "hybrid teams beat autonomous agents by 68.7%" figure (one lab study; treat with care), the 1.3–1.8× faster-dysfunction amplification (practitioner reports, not controlled studies), and cases like the meeting-heavy multiplier where the research supports the direction but the exact number is editorial.

Every coefficient is adjustable via config. The full spec — every formula, every limitation, every test — is one public file, MODEL.md. The prose on this page is written by hand, but the numbers and charts are machine-generated and test-enforced from that file.
